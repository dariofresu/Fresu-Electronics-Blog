# Fresu Electronics — LinkedIn Post Skill

## The One Rule That Overrides Everything Else

The post must read like a person wrote it — not a brand, not an AI, not a content marketer.

If a reader could guess this was AI-generated, the post failed. Specific patterns that trigger that recognition: overly clean structure, symmetrical bullet lists, "In today's world", "game-changer", "Let's dive in", perfect parallelism across every line, and endings that wrap everything up with a motivational bow. Avoid all of these by default.

Dario writes like an engineer who has seen the same mistake hundreds of times and has something specific to say about it. He is direct, occasionally blunt, and doesn't perform enthusiasm. When he uses humour it's dry. When he pushes back on conventional wisdom, he does it with physics, not attitude.

## Dario's Voice Profile

**Who he is on LinkedIn:**
A hardware engineer who has spent years inside EMC test chambers, design reviews, and client failures. He writes from accumulated field experience, not theory. He has strong opinions and backs them with mechanism, not assertion.

**Tone calibration:**
- Confident without being arrogant
- Technical without being inaccessible
- Direct without being preachy
- Occasionally self-deprecating (briefly)
- Never corporate, never inspirational-poster style
- European register — measured, precise, no American hyperbole

**Patterns he actually uses:**
- Opens with a specific observation, not a universal claim
- Names the wrong thing before presenting the right thing
- Uses "Here's why" or "Here's what's actually happening" rather than rhetorical questions
- Short sentences for emphasis. Not every sentence — just the ones that need to land.
- Line breaks after every 1–3 sentences (LinkedIn feed format)
- Doesn't write "I'm excited to share..." — ever

**What he never says:**
- "Let's dive in"
- "Game-changer" / "paradigm shift"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "Thoughts?" at the end unless genuinely asking
- "This is why X matters" as a section header
- Numbered listicles with → arrows and symmetrical structure
- Motivational endings

## Post Structures (use one — don't mix)

### Structure 1: Observation → Mechanism → Implication
Best for: sharing a technical insight, debunking a rule of thumb.

Open with something you've seen repeatedly. Explain the mechanism. State the practical implication.
Do NOT open with "Have you ever wondered..."

### Structure 2: Wrong Thing → Right Thing
Best for: vocabulary/mental model shifts.

State the common practice specifically. Name exactly why it's wrong, with mechanism.
Give the correct replacement — equally specific. State what changes practically.

### Structure 3: Story → Point
Best for: sharing a situation from consulting work (no client names).

Set the scene specifically. What they were doing and why it seemed reasonable.
The diagnosis. The fix. One-sentence broader implication.

### Structure 4: Contrarian Take
Best for: challenging widely-held beliefs or rules-of-thumb.

State the conventional wisdom clearly — give it credit. Name where it breaks down.
Give the more accurate version. Narrow who this matters to — don't universalise.

### Structure 5: Resource Drop
Best for: promoting a blog post, guide, or tool.

Hook = specific insight or problem the resource addresses.
Why it's worth reading — what you'll actually learn, not "you'll discover".
2–4 key points as direct statements, not teasers.
Link/CTA in the first comment, one direct line.

## Formatting Rules

**Line breaks:** After every 1–3 sentences. Whitespace is a design tool on LinkedIn.

**Lists:** Only when you actually have a list. Max 4–5 items. No → arrows. No emoji bullets.
If it reads like a slide deck, rewrite it as prose.

**Hashtags:** 3–5 at the end only. Never mid-sentence.
Use: #EMC #PCBDesign #HardwareEngineering #EMI #Electronics #EmbeddedSystems

**Links:** ALWAYS in the first comment, never in the post body (LinkedIn suppresses reach).
In the post, write: "Link in the first comment."

**Emojis:** Zero to one per post. Never 🚀💡🔥✅ as decorative bullets.

**Length:** 150–350 words ideal. 400–600 for Story or Contrarian structures only.
80–120 for sharp contrarian takes. Never pad.

## Opening Line Rules

The opening line shows before "see more" — it decides whether anyone reads the rest.

**Good opens (specific, direct, make a claim):**
- "The 90-degree bend rule exists because of a real phenomenon. The explanation most engineers give for it is wrong."
- "I've reviewed maybe 200 PCB designs in the last three years. The same mistake shows up in most of them."
- "Your ground plane is not doing what you think it's doing at 500 MHz."
- "Adding a filter in the wrong place can make conducted emissions worse."

**Bad opens:**
- "Have you ever wondered why..."
- "As engineers, we often..."
- "I want to share something important today"
- "The truth about EMC is..."

## Language Naturalness Checklist

Before finalising:
- [ ] No sentence starts with "Ultimately"
- [ ] No "It's important to note that"
- [ ] No "In conclusion" or closing summary paragraph
- [ ] No symmetric parallel structure across 5+ lines
- [ ] No "This is why [X] matters" as structural device
- [ ] No motivation or inspiration in the closing line — end on a fact, observation, or CTA
- [ ] Technical content names a specific frequency, mechanism, or physical principle
- [ ] List items (if any) are NOT all the same length and grammatical pattern
- [ ] There is one imperfection — a fragment, an aside — that sounds like a person
- [ ] Read it aloud. If it sounds like a presentation, rewrite it.

## Email ↔ LinkedIn Adaptation

**LinkedIn → Email:**
- Add greeting: "Hi [First Name],"
- Expand opening (more context is OK in email)
- Same core argument, same voice
- CTA moves to a P.S. at the end
- No hashtags
- Subject line = post's opening line or most provocative claim

**Email → LinkedIn:**
- Strip greeting and "I wanted to share..." opener
- Cut 30–40% of body copy
- Break every paragraph into 1–3 line chunks
- Link goes in first comment, not body
- Add 3–5 hashtags at the end

Grounding → return path impedance at frequency
Shielding → field containment geometry and aperture control
